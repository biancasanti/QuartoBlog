Loading the data

For this tutorial we’ll be using a dataset included in the nlmeU package. This data comes from the Study of Structural Improvement (SII) Project, which aimed to assess match achievement scores for first and third graders in randomly selected classrooms at elementary schools across the US (Hill, Rowan, and Ball 2005). The dataset contains data from 1,190 first graders sampled from 312 classrooms in 107 schools.

This dataset is structured hierarchically: each student belongs to a classroom, and each classroom belongs to a school. The 12 variables in the dataset are:

  • school-level variables:
    • schoolid: ID number for the school (factor)
    • housepov: percentage of households below the poverty line in the school’s neighborhood (numeric)
  • classroom-level variables:
    • classid: ID number for the classroom (factor)
    • yearstea: teacher’s years of experience for teaching, in first grade (numeric)
    • mathknow: how well the teacher knows the first grade math curriculum, with higher scores indicating more knowledge (numeric)
    • mathprep: how many courses on first grade math knowledge the teacher follows (numeric)
  • student-level variables:
    • childid: ID number for the student (factor)
    • sex: the student’s sex (factor with labeled levels 1 (M) and 2 (F))
    • minority: whether or not the student has minority status (factor with labeled levels 1 (Mnrt=No) and 2 (Mnrt=Yes))
    • mathkind: the student’s math score in the spring of kindergarten (numeric, integer)
    • mathgain: the student’s grain in math score from the spring of kindergarten to the spring of first grade (numeric, integer)
    • ses: the student’s socioeconomic status (numeric)

Over the course of this tutorial, we’ll be exploring the effect of the school-level, classroom-level, and student-level variables on the students’ gain in math scores between kindergarten and first grade.

Why not use simple linear models?

When looking at this dataset, we could form a simple naive hypothesis: for example, that the scores students get in kindergarten are related to the scores they get in first grade. We can make a scatter plot for these two variables in a smaller subset of the data (just for visualization purposes) to see if this naive hypothesis matches the data.

SII_toy_data <- SIIdata %>%
  slice_head(n = 200)

ggplot(SII_toy_data, aes(x = mathkind, y = mathgain)) +
  geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) +
  labs(title = "Change in math scores based on kindergarten scores", 
       x = "Kindergarten math achievement score", 
       y = "Gain in math scores from kindergarten to first grade")
`geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

Indeed, that seems to be the case! The linear line of best fit shows a correlation between kindergarten scores and score gain, with students that scored higher in kindergarten having a lower gain from kindergarten to first grade. This makes sense: higher scores leave less room for improvement, while students with initially lower scores have much more potential to improve. However, it could also be possible to have a classroom environment where the teacher is better at supporting already high-achieving students, while lower-achieving students don’t get as much support and therefore improve less.

Is more improvement for initially lower-scoring students a global phenomenon, or is the opposite effect also possible? To begin answering this question, we can try looking at the same plot as above, but this time visualize the different classrooms and add in a trend line for each classroom.

ggplot(SII_toy_data,

  geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) +
  labs(title = "Change in math scores based on kindergarten scores", 
       subtitle = "Grouping by classroom",
       x = "Kindergarten math achievement score", 
       y = "Gain in math scores from kindergarten to first grade") +
    theme(legend.position = "none")
`geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

When we group the data this way, the picture that emerges is a bit more complicated: some classrooms still show the overall effect, with lower initial scores being correlated with more score gain. Some classrooms show the effect even more strongly with steeper downwards trend lines. However, some classrooms show the opposite effect: a higher initial score is correlated with more gain. This suggests that the relationship between kindergarten scores and score gain in first grade is not as simple as it may initially seem, and it might depend on some classroom-level variables, such as how effective the teacher is at teaching first grade math.

Another naive hypothesis we might form is that children from higher socioeconomic backgrounds (high SES) might have a higher gain in math scores. Once again, we can explore this hypothesis with a scatter plot in our subset of the data.

ggplot(SII_toy_data, aes(x = ses, y = mathgain)) +
  geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) +
  labs(title = "Change in math scores based on kindergarten scores",

       y = "Gain in math scores from kindergarten to first grade")
`geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

If we plot the score gain against SES within the same subset of the data, however, this doesn’t seem to be the case: the linear trend line for this correlation is basically flat. This might be because there is no correlation between SES and math score gain, but it might also be because the correlation between these factors is variable. For example, it could be that some school districts have more resources to support lower-income students and therefore have more flat or even decreasing trend lines, but in other school districts with less resources lower-income students aren’t able to increase their score as much. Once again, we can begin to uncover this variation by plotting the data with different trend lines for each school.

       aes(x = ses, y = mathgain, color = schoolid)) +
  geom_point() +
  labs(title = "Change in math scores based on kindergarten scores", 
       subtitle = "Grouping by school",
       x = "Socioeconomic status",

`geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

While the overall correlation was pretty flat, plotting the trend lines for each school shows a lot more variation: in some schools SES is positively correlated with score increases, in others the correlation is flat, and in one district the correlation is negative, so that higher income students improve their scores less. Once again, grouping the data revealed substantial variation, this time at the level of the school, which suggests that the relationship between SES and math score gain might be influenced by school-level variables.

These examples illustrate a common issue with using simple linear models to explain relationships in complicated, real-world datasets: simple linear models assume each data point is independent, while in the real world data points are often grouped and interdependent. Simple linear models can’t account for the fact that students in the same classroom are probably more similar than students in different classrooms, and classrooms in the same school are probably more similar than classrooms in different schools. They also can’t account for the variation that exists between groups: each grouping variable (classroom and school) can have different means for each variable and different slopes for relationships between variables.

Mixed effects models allow us to deal with this complexity in a dataset: they are made for dealing with data points that are not independent and are instead clustered or nested. Because of that, they can capture more of the meaningful variation in complex datasets like this and produce much more accurate predictions. Using mixed effects models is preferable when dealing with datasets that feature nested designs (such as the SII dataset we’re using here), repeated measures for each participant, longitudinal data, or generally complex designs.

Manually building a mixed effects model

Starting with a simple linear model

In a simple linear model, a linear correlation is fit to the data and each data point is assumed to be independent, so any variation between groups is assumed to be normally distributed error.

In a subset of the data, we could fit a linear model for the relationship between kindergarten math scores and math score gain. We can use the lm function to fit a simple linear model that predicts math score gain based on kindergarten math scores (mathgain ~ ses); we are assuming that all other variation in the data is random error.

# take a smaller subset (n = 100) for visualization purposes
SII_toy_data <- SII_toy_data %>% slice_head(n = 100)

toy_lm_kind <- lm(mathgain ~ mathkind, data = SII_toy_data)
summary(toy_lm_kind)

Adding random intercepts

One of the way that mixed effects models can account for variation between grouping variables is by adding random intercepts. Instead of assuming one intercept for the line of best fit across all the data, we can allow the intercept to vary between groups, so that each group has its own intercept (but the same slope). This means that the variance in intercepts (or conceptually, in the average math score gain) between different classrooms is no longer modeled as error, but is instead modeled as a distribution of deviations from the overall mean intercept.

To do this in R, we can use the lmer function from the lmerTest package to fit a mixed effects model that still predicts math score gain based on kindergarten math scores (mathgain ~ mathkind), but this time adds in a term for random intercepts for each classroom (1 | classid). This model has two components:

  • Fixed (or constant) effects - population-level effects that persist across groups. In our case, this is the population-level intercept and slope for the effect of kindergarten math scores.
  • Random effects - effects that vary for each group and model additional variation as a distribution around the fixed effects. In our case, we are adding in one random effect: random intercepts for each classroom.
toy_intercept_kind <- lmer(mathgain ~ mathkind + (1 | schoolid), data = SII_toy_data)
summary(toy_intercept_kind)

Adding random slopes

The other random effect that we can account for with a mixed effects model is different slopes for each group. Instead of assuming that the slope of the predicted line must be the same across all groups, we can add in a random slopes term and allow the slope to vary between groups. This means that the variance in slopes (conceptually, the relationship between kindergarten scores and score gains) between different classrooms is no longer modeled as an error, but is instead considered a distribution of deviations from the overall population-level slope.

We can add this change to our previous model, so that now each group has both its own intercept and its own slope. With the lmer package, we can now fit a mixed effects model that predicts math score gain based on kindergarten math scores (mathgain ~ mathkind) and adds in terms for random intercepts for each classroom (1 | classid) and random slopes for the effect of kindergarten math scores by classroom (mathkind | classid), which are combined into (1 + mathkind | classid).

toy_intercept_slopes_kind <- lmer(mathgain ~ mathkind + (1 + mathkind | schoolid), data = SII_toy_data)

Mixed effects modeling in R

Now that we know the added features of mixed effects models, random effects (random intercepts and random slopes), we can go over how to implement mixed effects modeling in R. In this tutorial, we’ll be using the lmerTest package to implement all mixed effects models. The general workflow for modeling data with mixed effects models is:

  1. Exploring relationships between variables
  2. Implementing model(s)
  3. Model comparison
  4. Interpreting model outputs

Let’s go through these steps one by one.

Exploring relationships between variables

Visualizing data is always a helpful first step before doing statistical modeling. In our case, without any a priori hypotheses or analysis plan, visualizing the data and relationships between variables also helps us determine what variables should be included in our statistical models.

One helpful visualization (that we’ve already used above) during data exploration is a scatterplot, which we can use to understand the relationships between continuous variables. Since we’re interested in modeling the gain in math scores between kindergarten and first grade, we can create scatterplots with the mathgain variable on the y axis and other continuous variables on the x axis. Let’s start with kindergarten math scores (mathkind), this time looking at the full sample instead of a subset.

ggplot(SIIdata, aes(x = mathkind, y = mathgain)) +
  geom_point() +
  labs(x = "Kindergarten math achievement score", 
       y = "Gain in math scores from kindergarten to first grade")

From this, we can see that the full sample has a fairly even spread in both math gain scores and in kindergarten scores (with the exception of some very low potential outlier values for kindergarten scores). The two variables still seem to be negatively correlated in the full sample, which means kindergarten scores are likely a useful variable to include in our models.

Now that we’ve visualized some of the variance in our data, we can move on to building mixed effects models to model our data.

Implementing different models

In order to implement a mixed effects model, we need to decide which fixed and random effects to include in our model structure. The fixed effects represent individual predictors, while random effects reflect effects at the group (classroom or school) level.

In our case, we can build a model that predicts the gain in math scores from all student-level predictors (sex, minority status, kindergarten math scores, and SES), the school-level predictor of percentage of households under the poverty level, and one classroom-level predictor that we think might make an impact: years of teaching experience for the classroom teacher. If we assume each of these predictors acts independently, the fixed effects for our model are sex + minority + mathkind + ses + housepov + yearstea.

We can also add in random intercepts for both of the grouping levels we have in our dataset, classrooms and schools, since we expect each to have different baseline levels of gain in math scores. That means that the random effects structure for our first model is (1 | classid) + (1 | schoolid).

Altogether, this first mixed effects model predicts gain in math scores from the combination of fixed and random effects: mathgain ~ sex + minority + mathkind + ses + housepov + yearstea + (1 | classid) + (1 | schoolid). We can call it like so using the lmer function:

model1 <- lmer(mathgain ~ sex + minority + mathkind + ses + housepov + yearstea 
               + (1 | classid) + (1 | schoolid), 
               data = SIIdata)

We can then use the summary() function to see the model output.

We could complicate this model a bit if we have reason to believe that the slopes for some effects could also vary by group. For instance, we might think that the relationship between kindergarten scores and score gain might depend on classroom-level effects, such as teacher characteristics. We could also think that the relationship between SES and math gain could vary between schools. To add these hypotheses to our model, we need to add random slopes for these predictors, which means that our full random effects structure is (1 + mathkind | classid) + (1 + ses | schoolid). We can implement and summarize this second model using the following syntax:

model2 <- lmer(mathgain ~ sex + minority + mathkind + yearstea + ses + housepov  
               + (1 + mathkind | classid) + (1 + ses| schoolid), 
               data = SIIdata)

But what if we don’t think that each of the fixed effects predictors has an independent effect? For instance, we might think that the relationship between kingergarten scores and gain in math scores depends on the years of teaching experience that the teacher has, or that the effect of SES on score gain is moderated by the poverty level in the notebook. In order to implement this, we can add interactions to our fixed effects: an interaction between kindergarten scores and years of teaching experience, mathkind * yearstea, and an interaction between SES and neighborhood poverty levels, ses * housepov. We don’t need to specify the fixed effects for each of these variables separately, since the syntax for interaction effects also allows us to calculate the independent effects. With this addition, our third model can be called like this:

model3 <- lmer(mathgain ~ sex + minority + mathkind * yearstea + ses * housepov  
               + (1 | classid) + (1 + ses | schoolid), 
               data = SIIdata)

Model comparison

Now that we know how to implement mixed effects models, we’re left with the very important question of how to choose which model fits the data the best. As you’ve seen above, we can create several different models to predict one outcome with the same set of predictors in a complex dataset like this one. How do we know which one to use?

One way to resolve this issue is to specify the model ahead of time, before running the analysis, based on your hypotheses (for example, in a preregistration). However, even prespecified models might fail to converge, or you might need to justify using a more complex or simpler model than a different option you might have chosen.

To help in our decision-making, we can use the test_likelihoodratio function from the easystats package, which performs a likelihood ratio test to test which model is a better (more likely) explanation for the data. Crucially, however, this test only works on nested models, which means that all the predictors for one model (the smaller one) should be present in the bigger model.

Let’s use this test to compare two of the models we created earlier: one with just random intercepts by classroom and school (model1) and one with an added random slope for SES by school (model 2).

test_likelihoodratio(model1, model2, estimator = "REML")
Some of the nested models seem to be identical and probably only vary in
  their random effects.
# Likelihood-Ratio-Test (LRT) for Model Comparison (REML-estimator)

Name   |           Model | df | df_diff | Chi2 |     p
------------------------------------------------------
model1 | lmerModLmerTest | 10 |         |      |      
model2 | lmerModLmerTest | 12 |       2 | 1.23 | 0.540

Here, we are using the REML estimator, since the models we are comparing only differ in random effects structure but have identical fixed effects structure. The likelihood ratio test indicates that the addition of random slopes by school for SES did not significantly improve model fit (p = 0.540). That means we can drop the random slope and just use the model with the random intercepts.

We can also compare our model1 to slightly more complex models that keep the same random effects structure but include interactions for in the fixed effects.

Let’s create a model4 with an interaction between mathkind and yearstea, a model5 with an interaction between ses and housepov, and a model6 with both interaction terms.

model4 <- lmer(mathgain ~ sex + minority + mathkind * yearstea + ses + housepov

The random effects portion of the model output shows us how much variance each random effect accounts for. Here, we can see that both of our group random intercepts account for a good amount of the variance in the model, but there is still substantial residual variance.

The fixed effects portion of the model output shows the estimate, standard error, and p values for each of our fixed effects coefficients. For a continuous predictor, like kindergarten math scores, we can interpret the estimate as showing us the estimated change in our outcome (gain in math scores) based on a one-unit increase in the predictor: in this case, scoring one more point in kindergarten predicts -0.47 less points of gain by first grade. For a categorical predictor, like minority status, the estimate shows the estimated change in the outcome for a certain predictor category relative to the reference category. For minority status, the reference level is “no”, so we can interpret the estimate as showing that minority students on average have 7.72 less points of math score gains compared to non-minority students. By looking at the p values, we can understand which of our predictors were significant: in this case, minority status, kindergarten scores, and SES all significantly predict the gain in scores between kindergarten and first grade.

Conclusion

In this tutorial, we learned what linear mixed effects models are, when and why we need to use them, how random effects work, and how to implement statistical analyses with linear mixed effects models in R, using the SII dataset of student math scores as an example.

Linear mixed effects models are a powerful statistical tool, especially for complex datasets with nesting structure or repeated measurements, and learning how to implement them is helpful for performing analyses in complex datasets. While powerful, these models still operate within the frequentist framework and have several of the limitations associated with linear regression, so depending on your research questions and design, alternative methods like hierarchical Bayesian models might be more appropriate.
